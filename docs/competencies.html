Exploring Systems Like a Scientist

Scope Table — Cognitive Architecture Revision

C1 = Evaluates (Knowledge) → C2 = Supports (Skill) → C3 = Explains Systems (Ability)

Evidence Statements — Line Items
Claim Structure
Focus: What are the parts of a scientific claim and how do they fit together?
  • Defining what is a scientific claim
  • Identify the role of scientific claims in asking questions and sharing ideas
  • Identify the components of a scientific claim
  • Identify the claim, supporting evidence, and underlying assumptions
  • Distinguish between statements of fact, opinion, and inference
Evidence Types
Focus: What kinds of evidence exist and what assumptions do they carry?
  • Distinguishing quantitative, qualitative, and observational evidence
  • Identifying underlying assumptions in claims and evidence
Scientific vs. Non-Scientific Claims
Focus: What makes a claim scientific, and what doesn't?
  • Distinguishing scientific claims from non-scientific claims (e.g., pseudoscience and misinformation)
  • Differentiating scientific claims from scientific knowledge
  • Recognizing how scientific knowledge evolves over time
  • Recognizing that scientific claims are testable and open to revision
  • Considering the historical context of scientific discovery
  • Understanding the role of peer review in validating scientific findings
Bias and Misleading Representations
Focus: How can claims or sources be misleading, and how do you spot it?
  • Identify bias, misinformation, or misleading representations
  • Evaluate credibility and limitations of sources at an introductory level
  • Identify cherry-picked data or evidence that ignores contradictory findings
  • Recognize emotional appeals and false urgency used to persuade
  • Identify absolute claims that oversimplify complex evidence
  • Understanding potential biases in tech-generated responses or datasets
Evidence Quality
Focus: Is the evidence appropriate, sufficient, and relevant to the claim?
  • Analyze evidence used to support existing scientific claims
  • Identify missing, weak, or misapplied evidence
  • Assess whether evidence is relevant, credible, and sufficient
  • Recognize different types of evidence and when each is appropriate
  • Identify missing or underrepresented evidence
  • Consider whether the evidence applies to the population, setting, or context
  • Predict what kinds of evidence would be relevant
  • Check whether the evidence directly measures what the claim asserts
  • Check whether appropriate comparisons or controls were used
  • Recognizing the role of controls in evaluating whether evidence supports a claim
  • Identify missing scientific, societal, or ethical considerations
  • Considering whether a claim has been peer-reviewed
  • Using digital tools (including AI) to find or evaluate evidence
Reasoning Quality
Focus: Can the student understand the claim and assess whether the logic connecting evidence to it holds up?
  • Interpret scientific claims using basic scientific models or concepts
  • Restate the claim in plain language and identify the basic scientific concept/model
  • Explain how the claim is supposed to work using that concept
  • Explain why a claim is or is not supported based on available evidence
  • Check whether there is a plausible mechanism connecting the evidence to the claim
  • Apply simple evaluation criteria to assess scientific reasoning
  • Use foundational science knowledge to critique claims
  • Identify key assumptions the claim depends on
  • Identify gaps, unsupported assumptions, or overgeneralizations in the reasoning
  • Recognize when evidence shows an association or trend rather than cause-effect
  • Recognizing spurious correlations
  • Apply evaluation skills within structured, scenario-based contexts
Data Interpretation
Focus: Can the student read and interpret evidence accurately?
  • Examine and interpret data or evidence related to claims
  • Reading graphs and tables accurately
  • Recognizing general trends (increasing, decreasing, no change)
  • Understanding what a data point or bar represents
  • Recognizing when data shows clear differences versus uncertain results
Claim Construction
Focus: How do you build a clear, structured scientific claim?
  • Constructing a scientific claim
  • Using the CER Framework for Constructing a Claim
  • Translating scientific language to plain language
Source Selection and Credibility
Focus: Where does the evidence come from and can it be trusted?
  • Considering bias, assumptions, and credibility of sources
  • Locating evidence through secondary sources accessible to non-specialists
  • Evaluating the credibility of secondary sources
  • Understanding how the type of source affects credibility
Evidence-to-Claim Reasoning
Focus: How do you connect evidence to a claim logically?
  • Connecting evidence to claims clearly
  • Explaining why evidence adequately supports a claim
  • Identifying when evidence does not adequately support a claim
  • Using scientific principles to explain why evidence supports a claim
  • Avoiding circular reasoning where evidence is restated as justification
  • Distinguishing between correlation and causation when connecting evidence to claims
Counterclaims and Public Impact
Focus: What happens when a claim is challenged, and what are the stakes?
  • Considering ethical implications of communicating scientific claims to the public
  • Not about ethics as content, but ethics as consequence
  • Aligns with misinformation and public impact examples
System Boundaries and Components
Focus: What is inside the system and what is outside it?
  • Define systems, components, and boundaries
  • Identify what is included in the system vs. outside it
  • Identify key components and their roles in the system
Interactions and Feedback
Focus: How do the parts of the system influence each other?
  • Identify interactions, feedback loops, and patterns
  • Describe how components influence one another
  • Recognize feedback loops in plain language (reinforcing or balancing)
  • Identify patterns or recurring behaviors in the system
  • Explain why single-cause explanations can be incomplete
  • Describe how relationships between parts shape outcomes
  • Explain the value of a systems perspective
Communicating System Understanding
Focus: Can the student explain how the system works clearly?
  • Communicate system understanding clearly
  • Explain system structure or behavior using straightforward language
  • Use simple visuals (e.g., labeled diagram, arrows showing influence)
Tracing Change Through Systems
Focus: What happens when something in the system changes?
  • Connect foundational scientific concepts to system behavior
  • Explain how interactions are more appropriate than cause-and-effect reasoning for a given system
  • Ask scientific questions about system behavior
  • Ask what influences what in the system
  • Ask what might change if a component changes
  • Explain how changes in one part influence other components over time
  • Trace how a change moves through the system step-by-step
  • Identify which components are most affected and why
  • Describe how feedback loops may amplify or reduce the effects
  • Explain ripple effects across connected components
Applying Concepts to Outcomes
Focus: How do scientific concepts help explain what is happening in the system?
  • Use concepts like cycles, energy flow, or change over time when relevant
  • Explain how the concept helps interpret what is happening in the system
  • Ask why outcomes may differ across contexts or conditions
  • Describe outcomes based on interactions among system components
  • Use the relationships in the system to explain outcomes (not isolated events)
  • Support explanations with scenario information
Consequences and Trade-offs
Focus: What are the intended and unintended effects of changes in the system?
  • Explain intended and unintended impacts of changes within a system
  • Identify intended outcomes of a decision or change
  • Identify unintended consequences or trade-offs
  • Acknowledge trade-offs and value-based considerations
  • Identify benefits and costs connected to decisions in the system
Human Choices and Institutions
Focus: How do people, norms, and institutions shape the system?
  • Consider cultural, societal, and ethical perspectives in systems analysis
  • Identify how human choices influence system behavior
  • Consider how beliefs, incentives, or norms shape decisions
  • Identify relevant societal or ethical considerations at an introductory level
  • Recognize how societies, cultures, and governments function within systems
  • Identify how policies, rules, or institutions affect system behavior
  • Describe how access and resources influence outcomes
Perspective and Equity
Focus: Who is affected, and how does perspective shape the analysis?
  • Explain why stakeholders may prioritize different outcomes
  • Consider how impacts differ across groups or perspectives
  • Identify who is affected differently and how
  • Explain how perspective changes what outcomes are emphasized
  • Communicate explanations in a clear, fair way across viewpoints
  • Recognize human-centered biases in systems analysis
  • Identify assumptions that shape interpretation
  • Notice common biases (e.g., focusing on short-term outcomes or single causes)
  • Reflect on how perspective influences interpretation and communication
  • Explain how one's perspective may affect conclusions
