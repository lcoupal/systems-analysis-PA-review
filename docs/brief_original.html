The Scenario

Read this background before reviewing the evidence.

Antibiotic resistance is a growing global health threat. Bacteria that were once easily treated with common antibiotics are becoming resistant, making infections harder—and sometimes impossible—to treat. Each year, resistant infections kill more than one million people worldwide and sicken millions more.

Antibiotics are used in many settings: doctors prescribe them to treat infections in patients, hospitals use them during surgeries and for critically ill patients, and farmers give them to livestock to prevent disease and promote growth. When antibiotics are used—whether in people or animals—bacteria are exposed to the drug. Most bacteria die, but some survive because they carry or develop genetic traits that make them resistant. These survivors multiply, and over time, resistant bacteria become more common.

The question of what drives resistance most is debated. Some experts point to the massive volume of antibiotics used in agriculture. Others argue that unnecessary prescriptions in hospitals and clinics are the bigger problem. Still others say no single cause dominates—that resistance is the result of many interacting factors across a complex system.

The evidence below comes from different types of sources—government agencies, scientific research, industry groups, and investigative reporting. As you read, consider: What does each source actually show? What are its limitations? And what position does the full body of evidence best support?

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one shows, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
World Health Organization — Antimicrobial Resistance Fact Sheet (2023)
International public health agency

The World Health Organization (WHO) identifies antimicrobial resistance as one of the top 10 global public health threats. According to the WHO, resistant infections directly cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year worldwide and contribute to nearly 5 million more.

The WHO states that resistance is accelerated by the overuse and misuse of antimicrobials in both human medicine and animal agriculture, as well as by poor infection prevention, lack of clean water and sanitation, and limited access to affordable medicines and diagnostics. The WHO does not identify any single sector as the primary driver. Instead, it describes resistance as a problem that “threatens the effective prevention and treatment of an ever-increasing range of infections” and calls for coordinated action across all sectors.

Key figure: 1.27 million deaths per year are directly caused by antibiotic-resistant infections globally.
2
U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) — Antibiotic Use in Food-Producing Animals
U.S. federal health agency

According to the CDC, approximately 70% of antibiotics sold in the United States that are classified as “medically important”—meaning they are also used to treat infections in humans—are sold for use in food-producing animals such as cattle, pigs, and poultry.

The majority of these antibiotics are not used to treat sick animals. Instead, they are added to feed or water to prevent disease in crowded conditions or, historically, to promote faster growth. The CDC warns that when medically important antibiotics are used in livestock, resistant bacteria can develop in animals and then spread to people through the food supply, through direct contact with animals, or through environmental pathways such as water contaminated by animal waste.

Key figure: ~70% of medically important antibiotics sold in the U.S. are used in food-producing animals, not people.
3
Journal of Antimicrobial Chemotherapy — Hospital Prescribing Practices Study (2022)
Peer-reviewed scientific research

Researchers analyzed antibiotic prescribing records from 120 hospitals across the United States and Europe. They found that between 30% and 50% of antibiotic prescriptions written in hospitals were either unnecessary (the patient did not have a bacterial infection) or inappropriate (the wrong antibiotic was chosen, or the treatment lasted longer than needed).

The study argues that over-prescribing in hospitals is a significant and underappreciated driver of resistance. Unlike agricultural use, hospital antibiotics are given directly to humans—meaning resistant bacteria that develop in hospital settings are already adapted to infect people. The researchers highlight drug-resistant infections such as MRSA and C. difficile as examples of resistance driven primarily by clinical overuse, not agricultural use.

Key figure: 30–50% of antibiotic prescriptions in hospitals were found to be unnecessary or inappropriate.
4
National Cattlemen’s Beef Association — Industry Position Statement
Livestock industry trade organization

The National Cattlemen’s Beef Association (NCBA) states that the livestock industry has made significant progress in reducing antibiotic use. Following the FDA’s 2017 Veterinary Feed Directive—which banned the use of medically important antibiotics for growth promotion in animals—total sales of antibiotics for agricultural use declined by 33% between 2015 and 2020.

The NCBA argues that the antibiotics still used in livestock are necessary for animal welfare: to treat sick animals and to prevent disease outbreaks in herds. The organization contends that resistance in livestock bacteria does not automatically transfer to the bacteria that infect humans, because these are often different species of bacteria with different resistance mechanisms. They point to human clinical overuse as the more direct threat to human health.

Key figure: Sales of antibiotics for use in food-producing animals declined 33% from 2015 to 2020 after FDA regulations.
5
Investigative Report: Antibiotic Runoff and Rural Health
Investigative journalism

An investigative team documented conditions in three rural counties located near large-scale poultry and swine farming operations. They found that waste from these operations—which contains antibiotics, antibiotic-resistant bacteria, and resistance genes—was entering local waterways through runoff, leaking waste lagoons, and land application of manure as fertilizer.

Health records showed that residents in these communities had 2.5 times the rate of antibiotic-resistant urinary tract infections compared to national averages. Notably, most affected residents had no direct contact with livestock. Researchers interviewed for the report said the finding suggests that resistant bacteria can spread to humans through environmental pathways—water and soil—not just through food or direct animal contact. The affected communities were predominantly low-income with limited access to healthcare.

Key figure: Residents near large livestock operations had 2.5x the national rate of resistant urinary tract infections.
6
The Lancet — Global Drivers of Antimicrobial Resistance: A Systematic Review (2023)
Peer-reviewed research (systematic review)

A team of international researchers reviewed 412 studies on the causes of antimicrobial resistance published between 2010 and 2023. Their review identified five major interacting drivers of resistance:

(1) Overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human clinical settings
(2) Widespread antibiotic use in agriculture and livestock production
(3) Poor sanitation, water treatment, and infection control—especially in low-income countries
(4) A lack of new antibiotic development by pharmaceutical companies (no major new class of antibiotic has been developed since the 1980s)
(5) Patient behavior, including not finishing prescribed antibiotic courses and demanding antibiotics for viral infections

The authors concluded that no single sector or behavior can be identified as the primary cause. They argue that resistance is a “systems-level phenomenon” in which multiple drivers interact and reinforce each other. For example, agricultural antibiotic use creates environmental reservoirs (Driver 2), which are worsened by poor sanitation (Driver 3), while the lack of new drugs (Driver 4) means that once resistance emerges, there are fewer treatment alternatives. Framing one sector as the sole cause, the authors write, risks “directing resources toward incomplete solutions.”

Key figure: The review identified 5 major interacting drivers of resistance, with no single dominant cause across the 412 studies reviewed.

Your Task

A viral blog post claims that hospitals are killing patients, based on data showing that frequent hospital visitors have much higher death rates. The blogger’s data is real—but is the conclusion correct? Read the post, the scenario, and all evidence sources below. Then construct your own claim that better explains the pattern in the data.

  • Read the blog post, the scenario, and all six evidence sources carefully.
  • Evaluate the blogger’s claim — is the data accurate? Is the conclusion the data actually supports? Identify what is wrong with the blogger’s reasoning.
  • Construct your own claim — write a specific, evidence-based statement that better explains why people who visit hospitals more often have higher death rates.
  • Support your claim with evidence and reasoning — reference at least two sources. Explain the logical connection between your evidence and your claim. Identify the confounding variable(s) that make the blogger’s correlation misleading.
  • Address a counterclaim — the blogger could argue that hospitals DO carry real risks (infections, medical errors). Acknowledge this and explain why it still does not support the blogger’s conclusion.

The Scenario

Read the blog post and the background before reviewing the evidence.

Viral Blog Post — HealthTruthNow.com

“Exposed: Hospitals Are Killing Patients!”

I pulled the data myself and the numbers don’t lie. People who visit the hospital more than 10 times per year have a death rate that is EIGHT TIMES HIGHER than people who visit once a year or less. You read that right—8x higher.

Think about it. Every time you walk through those doors, you’re exposed to superbugs, medical errors, unnecessary procedures, and drugs with dangerous side effects. The system isn’t designed to keep you healthy. It’s designed to keep you coming back.

My advice? Stay away from hospitals unless you’re literally dying. The data is clear: the more you go, the more likely you are to die.

2.3 million views • 47,000 shares • 8,400 comments

This blog post went viral on social media, sparking fear and debate. Some readers said it confirmed their distrust of the healthcare system. Others pointed out that the blogger might be misinterpreting the data. A local news station picked up the story, and a hospital association issued a response.

The blogger’s core argument rests on a real statistical pattern: people who visit hospitals more frequently do have higher death rates. That part of the data is accurate. But the blogger makes a critical logical leap—concluding that hospital visits cause the higher death rates. This is an example of a spurious correlation: a statistical relationship between two variables that appears meaningful but is actually explained by a third factor.

In science, a correlation means two things tend to occur together. A causal claim goes further—it says one thing causes the other. But just because two things are correlated does not mean one causes the other. A confounding variable is a hidden third factor that is related to both variables and may be the real reason they appear connected. For example, ice cream sales and drowning deaths are correlated—not because ice cream causes drowning, but because both increase in summer (the confounding variable is hot weather).

Your job is to look at the full body of evidence and determine: What is the confounding variable that explains this correlation? Why does it make the blogger’s causal claim wrong? And what does the evidence actually show about the relationship between hospital visits and death rates?

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one shows, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
National Health Statistics Report — Hospital Utilization and Patient Outcomes (2023)
U.S. federal health statistics agency

The National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) publishes annual data on hospital utilization across the United States. Their 2023 report confirms the pattern the blogger cited: patients with 10 or more hospital visits per year have a mortality rate of approximately 24.1%, compared to 2.9% for those with one visit or fewer per year.

However, the report includes extensive demographic and clinical breakdowns that the blogger did not mention. Among patients with 10+ visits per year, 68% were over age 65, 71% had two or more chronic conditions (such as heart failure, kidney disease, or cancer), and 43% were receiving end-of-life or palliative care. Among patients with one visit or fewer, 82% were under age 50, and 89% had no chronic conditions.

The report states: “Frequency of hospital visits is strongly associated with underlying disease burden and age. These factors independently predict mortality regardless of healthcare utilization.”

Key figure: Among frequent hospital visitors (10+/year), 71% had two or more chronic conditions and 43% were in end-of-life care.
2
Journal of General Internal Medicine — Confounding in Observational Health Data (2021)
Peer-reviewed scientific research

This study was designed to test a common statistical trap: the assumption that correlation implies causation in health data. The researchers analyzed records of 1.2 million patients and built two models. The first model looked only at hospital visit frequency and death rates—mimicking the blogger’s approach. This model showed a strong positive correlation: more visits, higher death rates.

The second model controlled for the patients’ age, number of chronic conditions, and disease severity at baseline. When these confounding variables were accounted for, the correlation between hospital visits and death rates disappeared entirely. In fact, among patients with the same severity of illness, those who received more frequent hospital care had slightly lower mortality rates than those who received less care.

The researchers concluded: “The observed correlation between hospital utilization and mortality is almost entirely explained by underlying illness severity. When illness burden is held constant, increased hospital contact is associated with improved, not worsened, outcomes.”

Key figure: After controlling for age and illness severity, the visit-mortality correlation disappeared. Patients with more care had slightly lower death rates than similar patients with less care.
3
CDC Report: Healthcare-Associated Infections in U.S. Hospitals (2022)
U.S. federal health agency

The CDC tracks infections that patients acquire while receiving treatment in hospitals—known as healthcare-associated infections (HAIs). According to their 2022 report, approximately 1 in 31 hospital patients (about 3.2%) develops at least one HAI on any given day. The most common types include surgical site infections, catheter-related urinary tract infections, bloodstream infections, and C. difficile infections.

The report estimates that HAIs contribute to approximately 99,000 deaths per year in the United States. The CDC notes that many HAIs are preventable through better hand hygiene, sterilization procedures, and antibiotic stewardship, and that infection rates have declined significantly over the past decade due to targeted prevention programs.

The report does not claim that hospital visits cause death overall, but it does document that hospitals carry real, measurable risks—particularly for patients who are already ill, elderly, or immunocompromised.

Key figure: ~3.2% of hospitalized patients develop a healthcare-associated infection. HAIs contribute to ~99,000 deaths/year in the U.S.
4
American Hospital Association — Public Response to the Blog Post
Healthcare industry association

The American Hospital Association (AHA) released a statement responding directly to the viral blog post. The AHA stated that the blogger’s analysis “confuses correlation with causation in a way that could cause real harm to patients who need medical care.”

The statement explained that people who visit hospitals frequently are overwhelmingly those with serious, chronic, or terminal illnesses. “Hospitals do not make these patients sick,” the AHA wrote. “These patients come to hospitals because they are sick. Telling them to avoid care based on a misreading of statistics could delay treatment and cost lives.”

The AHA acknowledged that hospitals are not risk-free—citing medical errors and infections—but argued that the benefits of hospital care for patients with serious conditions far outweigh the risks. They cited data showing that survival rates for heart attacks, strokes, and cancer have all improved significantly over the past 20 years due to advances in hospital-based care.

Key figure: The AHA cited that 5-year cancer survival rates have improved from 50% to 68% over the past two decades, driven largely by hospital-based treatments.
5
Johns Hopkins Patient Safety Research — Medical Errors as a Cause of Death (2016, updated 2023)
University-based medical research

A widely cited study from Johns Hopkins University estimated that medical errors contribute to approximately 250,000 deaths per year in the United States, making it the third leading cause of death behind heart disease and cancer. Medical errors include misdiagnoses, surgical complications, medication mistakes, and communication failures between healthcare providers.

The researchers emphasized that their finding does not mean hospitals are inherently dangerous. Rather, they argued, the scale of the U.S. healthcare system—which handles hundreds of millions of patient encounters per year—means that even a small error rate produces a large absolute number of deaths. They compared it to aviation: millions of flights per year means that even an extremely low crash rate will produce some crashes.

A 2023 follow-up noted that the 250,000 figure has been debated by other researchers, with some arguing the true number is lower (closer to 100,000–150,000) when stricter definitions of “preventable” are applied. Regardless of the exact figure, the authors concluded that medical errors are a real and serious problem—but one best addressed by improving hospital systems, not by avoiding hospitals.

Key figure: Medical errors contribute to an estimated 100,000–250,000 deaths/year in the U.S. (the exact figure is debated).
6
Social Media Analysis: How the Blog Post Spread
Media and communications research

Researchers at a university media lab tracked how the blog post spread across social media platforms over a two-week period. The post was shared over 47,000 times. Analysis of the sharing patterns showed that 78% of shares occurred within the first 48 hours, before any fact-checks or expert responses had been published.

The researchers found that the most widely shared versions of the post were accompanied by comments like “I always knew hospitals were dangerous” and “Big Pharma doesn’t want you to see this.” Only 12% of users who shared the post also shared any of the subsequent fact-checks or expert corrections.

The study noted a measurable real-world effect: in the two weeks after the post went viral, three regional health systems reported a 9% decline in non-emergency hospital visits, and one emergency department reported patients arriving with delayed symptoms of treatable conditions who said they had avoided coming in because of what they read online.

Key figure: Non-emergency hospital visits declined 9% in affected regions in the two weeks following the viral post. Only 12% of people who shared the post also shared fact-checks.

A company executive has circulated an internal memo announcing a return-to-office mandate, claiming that remote workers are less productive. Read the memo, the scenario, and all evidence sources below. Then use the CER framework to construct and support your own scientific claim about whether remote work helps or hurts productivity.

  • Read the executive memo, the scenario, and all six evidence sources carefully.
  • Construct a claim — a specific, evidence-based statement about the relationship between work location and productivity.
  • Select evidence from the sources below that supports your claim. You must reference at least two sources.
  • Explain your reasoning — why does the evidence you selected support the claim you made? What is the logical connection?

The Scenario

Read the executive memo and background before reviewing the evidence.

Internal Company Memo — NovaTech Industries

Return to Office: Effective March 1

Team,

After careful review of our internal data, we have made the decision to require all employees to return to the office five days a week, effective March 1.

Our data shows that remote workers are 15% more productive by output metrics. However, we believe this number is misleading. Productivity is more than individual task completion. Since shifting to remote work, we have seen a 41% decline in cross-team project launches, a 23% drop in mentorship activity, and a significant erosion of company culture that is difficult to quantify but impossible to ignore.

Innovation happens when people are together. The ideas that built this company came from hallway conversations, whiteboard sessions, and the energy of being in the same room. We cannot afford to lose that.

We understand this is a significant change. But we believe it is the right one for NovaTech’s long-term competitiveness.

— Jordan Reeves, Chief Operating Officer, NovaTech Industries

The COVID-19 pandemic forced millions of workers into remote arrangements beginning in 2020. What started as an emergency measure became a years-long experiment. By 2023, roughly 30% of U.S. workers were working remotely at least part of the time, down from a peak of over 60% during lockdowns but far above the pre-pandemic rate of about 6%.

As companies decide whether to stay remote, go hybrid, or return fully to the office, the debate has centered on productivity. But “productivity” is harder to measure than it sounds. Different studies define it differently—some measure individual task output (lines of code written, calls completed, reports filed), while others measure team outcomes (projects launched, revenue generated, patents filed). The results can look very different depending on which definition is used.

The NovaTech memo illustrates this tension: individual output went up, but collaborative outcomes went down. Whether this means remote work “helps” or “hurts” productivity depends on what you count—and what you value.

The evidence below comes from academic research, company data, employee surveys, and economic analysis. As you read, consider: How does each source define “productivity”? What does each one actually measure? And what does the full body of evidence support?

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one measures, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
Stanford University — Randomized Controlled Trial of Remote Work (2023)
Peer-reviewed academic research

Stanford economist Nicholas Bloom conducted one of the most rigorous studies on remote work, partnering with a large multinational company to randomly assign 1,600 employees to either fully remote or fully in-office work for six months. Random assignment is important because it eliminates the possibility that differences in outcomes are caused by the types of people who choose remote work.

The study found that fully remote workers showed no significant difference in individual productivity compared to in-office workers when measured by performance reviews, output volume, and promotion rates. However, fully remote workers reported higher job satisfaction and were 33% less likely to quit during the study period, which the researchers noted has significant cost implications for employers (replacing an employee typically costs 50–200% of their annual salary).

Bloom noted an important caveat: the study measured individual performance, not team-level innovation or collaboration. He wrote that his findings “should not be interpreted as evidence that remote work has no effect on collaborative output, which we did not measure.”

Key figure: No significant individual productivity difference. Remote workers were 33% less likely to quit. The study did not measure collaboration or innovation.
2
Microsoft Research — Effects of Remote Work on Collaboration Among Information Workers (2022)
Corporate research / peer-reviewed publication

Microsoft analyzed communication data from over 61,000 of its own employees during the shift to remote work, tracking email, instant messages, scheduled meetings, and unscheduled calls. The study was published in the peer-reviewed journal Nature Human Behaviour.

The researchers found that remote work caused employees’ professional networks to become more siloed. Workers communicated more frequently with their immediate team but less frequently with people outside their team. Cross-group connections—the informal ties between departments that often spark new ideas—declined by approximately 25%. Synchronous communication (real-time conversations) dropped, replaced by asynchronous communication (emails and messages), which the researchers said is less effective for complex problem-solving and brainstorming.

The researchers concluded that remote work “may make it harder for employees to acquire and share new information across the network,” which could affect long-term innovation. They did not measure whether these network changes actually reduced innovation output—only that the communication patterns shifted in ways that prior research associates with less innovation.

Key figure: Cross-team communication declined ~25%. Workers became more siloed, communicating primarily within their immediate group.
3
Gallup Workplace Survey — Employee Engagement and Work Location (2024)
Large-scale employee survey

Gallup surveyed over 15,000 U.S. workers across industries about their work arrangements, engagement, and well-being. The survey found that hybrid workers (those splitting time between office and home, typically 2–3 days in-office) reported the highest levels of engagement of any group—higher than both fully remote and fully in-office employees.

Fully remote workers reported higher well-being and work-life balance but lower feelings of connection to company mission and fewer opportunities for career development. Fully in-office workers reported the lowest well-being scores and were most likely to say they were “actively looking for a new job” (34%, compared to 24% of hybrid workers and 19% of remote workers).

The survey also found significant differences by job type. Workers in roles requiring deep individual focus (software developers, analysts, writers) reported higher productivity when remote. Workers in roles requiring frequent real-time coordination (project managers, sales teams, healthcare workers) reported higher productivity in the office or in hybrid arrangements.

Key figure: Hybrid workers had the highest engagement. 34% of fully in-office workers were actively job-searching, compared to 19% of remote workers. Productivity effects varied significantly by job type.
4
National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) — Working Paper on Output Measurement (2023)
Economic research (working paper, not yet peer-reviewed)

Economists at the NBER analyzed productivity data from companies that tracked output metrics before and after shifting to remote work. They examined data from 45 mid-to-large firms across technology, finance, and professional services sectors.

The paper found that individual measurable output increased by 13% on average during remote work periods. However, the researchers issued a significant warning about how to interpret this figure. They found that much of the increase came from employees working longer hours—an average of 48.5 minutes more per day—rather than working more efficiently. When output was measured per hour worked, the increase was only 3%, and was not statistically significant in most firms.

The researchers also noted a “measurement bias”: remote work makes it easier to track individual, quantifiable tasks (emails sent, tickets closed, code committed) and harder to track less visible contributions (mentoring junior employees, informal knowledge sharing, contributing to team morale). They cautioned that “what we can measure easily may not be what matters most.”

Key figure: Individual output up 13%, but remote workers worked 48.5 more minutes/day. Per-hour productivity increase was only ~3% and not statistically significant in most firms.
5
Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) — Return-to-Office Mandate Outcomes Report (2024)
Professional association research

SHRM tracked outcomes at 142 companies that implemented return-to-office (RTO) mandates between 2022 and 2024. The report documented what happened after companies required employees to come back to the office full-time.

Within six months of implementing an RTO mandate, the companies experienced an average voluntary turnover increase of 14%. The departures were not evenly distributed: senior employees and high performers were the most likely to leave, followed by women and caregivers. Hiring costs increased by an average of $18,000 per replacement due to a tighter labor market in which competing companies offered flexible arrangements.

The report found no measurable improvement in productivity, revenue, or profitability at the companies that mandated a full return to office compared to industry peers that maintained hybrid or remote options. The report did find that companies with RTO mandates reported modest improvements in onboarding speed for new hires (new employees ramped up about 15% faster with in-person training) and in manager satisfaction with team visibility.

Key figure: 14% increase in voluntary turnover after RTO mandates, with senior employees most likely to leave. No measurable productivity improvement compared to hybrid/remote peers.
6
University of Pittsburgh — Study on Return-to-Office Mandates and Firm Performance (2024)
Peer-reviewed academic research

Researchers at the University of Pittsburgh analyzed financial performance data from 137 companies in the S&P 500 that announced return-to-office mandates between 2020 and 2024. They compared each company’s revenue, profitability, and stock performance to industry-matched peers that did not mandate a full return.

The study found no significant improvement in financial performance among companies that issued RTO mandates compared to matched peers. However, there was a statistically significant decline in employee satisfaction scores at RTO companies. The researchers concluded that RTO mandates “do not appear to improve firm value” and suggested that the mandates may be driven more by executive preferences and a desire for control than by evidence of productivity gains.

The researchers acknowledged limitations: financial performance is influenced by many factors beyond work location, and the study period overlapped with broader economic disruption. They also noted that some effects of in-person work—such as long-term innovation—may take years to appear in financial data.

Key figure: No significant financial performance improvement at S&P 500 companies with RTO mandates vs. matched peers. Employee satisfaction declined significantly at RTO companies.

Harvard Business School — Platform Economics and Restaurant Profitability (2023)
Peer-reviewed academic research

Researchers at Harvard Business School analyzed financial data from over 5,000 independent restaurants across 12 U.S. cities between 2019 and 2023. They compared restaurants that partnered with delivery platforms to similar restaurants that did not.

1
Tennessee STAR Experiment — The Landmark Study (1985–1989)
Randomized controlled experiment (government-funded)

Project STAR (Student/Teacher Achievement Ratio) is the most cited study in class size research. The state of Tennessee randomly assigned over 11,000 students in kindergarten through grade 3 to either small classes (13–17 students), regular classes (22–26 students), or regular classes with a teacher’s aide. Random assignment makes this study unusually rigorous—it eliminates the possibility that results are caused by differences in students rather than class size.

Students in small classes scored significantly higher on standardized tests—an average gain of 4–8 percentile points. The benefits were largest for students from low-income families and Black students, who gained 7–10 percentile points. Follow-up studies found that students who had been in small classes in early grades were more likely to graduate high school and attend college.

However, the study had important limitations. It was conducted in Tennessee in the 1980s, and some researchers question whether the results generalize to different states, demographics, and eras. The study also found that adding a teacher’s aide to a regular-sized class produced no measurable benefit—only actually reducing class size helped.

Key figure: Small classes (13–17 students) produced 4–8 percentile point gains. Low-income and Black students gained 7–10 points. Teacher aides in regular classes had no effect.
2
California Class Size Reduction — A Cautionary Tale (1996–2002)
State policy evaluation (government research)

Inspired by the STAR results, California invested $1.5 billion to reduce K–3 class sizes statewide from an average of 29 to 20 students. It was the largest class size reduction effort in U.S. history. Researchers evaluated the program’s impact over six years.

The results were far smaller than expected. California students gained only 1–2 percentile points on standardized tests—a fraction of the STAR results. Researchers identified a critical reason: to fill the tens of thousands of new teaching positions, schools had to hire large numbers of inexperienced and underqualified teachers. The quality of instruction in the smaller classes was lower than in the larger classes they replaced.

The effect was worst in high-poverty schools, which lost their experienced teachers to wealthier districts that could offer better pay and working conditions. The policy intended to help disadvantaged students but actually widened the achievement gap in the short term because experienced teachers migrated to affluent schools.

Key figure: $1.5 billion investment produced only 1–2 percentile point gains (vs. 4–8 in STAR). Hiring surge diluted teacher quality. High-poverty schools lost experienced teachers.

National Bureau of Economic Research — The Returns to Education Spending (2023)
Economic research (peer-reviewed)

NBER economists analyzed how different education investments compare in terms of student achievement gains per dollar spent. They examined class size reduction, teacher quality improvement programs, high-dosage tutoring, early childhood education, and technology integration across hundreds of studies.

The analysis found that class size reduction costs 2–3 times more per percentile point of achievement gain than investing in teacher professional development or high-dosage tutoring programs. Specifically, spending $4 million on reducing class sizes produced approximately 2–5 percentile points of gain, while spending the same amount on intensive tutoring produced 8–15 percentile points, and spending it on teacher coaching and development produced 5–9 percentile points.

The researchers emphasized that class size reduction is not ineffective—it does produce gains. But given limited budgets, it is one of the least cost-effective ways to improve student outcomes. They wrote: “The question is not whether smaller classes help, but whether they help more than what we give up to afford them.”

MIT Sloan — The Spread of True and False News Online (2018, updated 2023)
Peer-reviewed academic research

MIT researchers analyzed 126,000 stories shared on Twitter (now X) by approximately 3 million users over 10 years. This is one of the largest studies ever conducted on how information spreads on social media.

Nature — Randomized Experiment on Algorithmic vs. Chronological Feeds (2023)

A tech blogger claims that strict password policies cause more security breaches, based on data showing that companies with stricter requirements have more breaches. The blogger’s data is real—but is the conclusion correct? Read the post, the scenario, and all evidence sources below. Then construct your own claim that better explains the pattern in the data.

  • Read the blog post, the scenario, and all six evidence sources carefully.
  • Evaluate the blogger’s claim — is the data accurate? Is the conclusion the data actually supports? Identify what is wrong with the blogger’s reasoning.

The Scenario

Read the blog post and background before reviewing the evidence.

Tech Blog — CyberSkeptic.io

“Password Complexity Is a Scam: Strict Policies INCREASE Breaches”

I analyzed publicly reported breach data from 500 companies over three years. Companies with the strictest password requirements—12+ characters, uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols, and forced changes every 60 days—had 3.2x MORE security breaches than companies with simple or no password policies.

Think about it. When you force people to create impossible-to-remember passwords and change them constantly, what do they do? They write them on sticky notes. They reuse the same password with a “1” added at the end. They click “Forgot Password” 10 times a day. Complexity breeds insecurity.

The cybersecurity industry has sold us a lie for decades. Strict password policies don’t protect us—they make us LESS safe. It’s time to ditch complexity requirements entirely.

890,000 views • 12,000 shares • Featured on HackerNews front page

This blog post went viral in the tech community, with many commenters saying it validated their frustration with password policies. IT departments at several companies reported employees forwarding the article as justification for relaxing security requirements. A CISO (Chief Information Security Officer) at a Fortune 500 company published a rebuttal, and cybersecurity researchers weighed in.

The blogger’s data is drawn from a real pattern: companies with strict password policies do report more security breaches. That correlation is accurate. But the blogger makes a causal claim—that strict policies cause more breaches. This is an example of a spurious correlation: a statistical relationship between two variables that appears meaningful but is actually explained by a third factor.

In science, a correlation means two things tend to occur together. A causal claim goes further—it says one thing causes the other. But just because two things are correlated does not mean one causes the other. A confounding variable is a hidden third factor that is related to both variables and may be the real reason they appear connected. For example, cities with more firefighters tend to have more fire damage—not because firefighters cause damage, but because larger cities have both more firefighters and more fires.

Your job is to look at the full body of evidence and determine: What confounding variable(s) explain this correlation? Why does it make the blogger’s causal claim wrong? And what does the evidence actually show about the relationship between password policies and security?

Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report (2024)
Industry research (annual security report)

The Verizon DBIR is one of the most respected annual reports on cybersecurity incidents, analyzing over 30,000 security incidents and 10,000 confirmed breaches across 94 countries. The 2024 report provides context that the blogger’s analysis omits.

The report found that 81% of hacking-related breaches involved stolen or weak credentials (passwords). However, it also found that the most common attack method was not password guessing—it was phishing (tricking users into revealing their passwords) and credential stuffing (using passwords stolen from one site to access another). These attacks work regardless of password complexity because the password is obtained directly from the user or from a different breach.

Critically, the report notes that companies that are larger, handle more sensitive data, and face more attack attempts are both more likely to adopt strict security policies AND more likely to experience breaches—because they are bigger targets. A small bakery with no password policy rarely gets hacked; a bank with the strictest possible policies gets attacked constantly.

NIST Special Publication 800-63B — Digital Identity Guidelines (2023 Update)
U.S. federal government technical standard

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) sets the authoritative guidelines for digital authentication used by U.S. government agencies and widely adopted by the private sector. In a significant 2023 update, NIST reversed its earlier recommendation on forced password changes.

NIST now recommends against requiring periodic password changes (e.g., every 60 or 90 days), finding that forced changes lead to predictable password patterns (“Summer2024!” becomes “Fall2024!”) that are easier for attackers to guess. NIST still recommends a minimum password length of 8 characters and screening against known compromised passwords, but does not recommend complex character requirements (mandatory symbols, mixed case).

However, NIST’s updated guidance strongly emphasizes that passwords alone are insufficient for securing accounts. The guidelines recommend multi-factor authentication (MFA)—requiring a second verification method like a code sent to a phone—as the most effective protection. NIST’s position is that the type of password policy matters less than whether MFA is in place.

Key figure: NIST now recommends against forced password changes and complex character requirements. But it strongly recommends MFA as the most effective protection—passwords alone are not enough.

Peer-reviewed academic research

Researchers at Carnegie Mellon’s cybersecurity lab studied how real users behave under different password policies. They analyzed password creation patterns from 50,000 users across organizations with varying policy strictness.

A 12-character complex password, even a predictable variant, took an estimated 200x longer to crack than a simple 6-character password. Google Security Blog — Effectiveness of Authentication Methods (2023)

Google published data on the effectiveness of different authentication methods at preventing account takeovers, based on analysis of billions of login attempts across Google’s services.

The data showed that password strength alone blocked only 35% of automated attack attempts and was essentially useless against targeted phishing attacks (where the attacker tricks the user into entering their password on a fake site). Key figure: Password strength alone blocked 35% of automated attacks and 0% of targeted phishing. SMS MFA blocked 96%. Hardware keys blocked 100%.

Analysis of the Blogger’s Dataset — Independent Security Researcher Review
Independent analysis

A group of independent security researchers obtained and re-analyzed the blogger’s dataset of 500 companies. They confirmed that the raw correlation was accurate: companies with the strictest password policies did report 3.2x more breaches. But they identified two critical confounding variables the blogger did not control for.

Confound #1: Company size and value as a target. Companies with strict password policies were overwhelmingly large enterprises (banks, healthcare systems, tech companies) that store high-value data and face constant, sophisticated attacks. Companies with simple or no password policies were overwhelmingly small businesses that are attacked far less frequently. The correlation was between being a high-value target and having both strict policies and more breaches.

Confound #2: Detection and reporting capability. Companies with strict security programs also had better monitoring and breach detection tools, and were subject to regulatory requirements (like HIPAA and PCI-DSS) that legally required them to publicly report breaches. Small companies with weak security often suffered breaches they never detected or were not required to report. The blogger’s data counted reported breaches, not actual breaches.

When the researchers controlled for company size and industry, the correlation between password policy strictness and breach frequency reversed: among companies of the same size and industry, those with stricter policies had fewer breaches, not more.

Key figure: Two confounding variables—company size/target value and detection/reporting capability—explained the entire correlation. When controlled, stricter policies = fewer breaches.
