The Scenario

Read this background before reviewing the evidence.

Antibiotic resistance is a growing global health threat. Bacteria that were once easily treated with common antibiotics are becoming resistant, making infections harder—and sometimes impossible—to treat. Each year, resistant infections kill more than one million people worldwide and sicken millions more.

Antibiotics are used in many settings: doctors prescribe them to treat infections in patients, hospitals use them during surgeries and for critically ill patients, and farmers give them to livestock to prevent disease and promote growth. When antibiotics are used—whether in people or animals—bacteria are exposed to the drug. Most bacteria die, but some survive because they carry or develop genetic traits that make them resistant. These survivors multiply, and over time, resistant bacteria become more common.

The question of what drives resistance most is debated. Some experts point to the massive volume of antibiotics used in agriculture. Others argue that unnecessary prescriptions in hospitals and clinics are the bigger problem. Still others say no single cause dominates—that resistance is the result of many interacting factors across a complex system.

The evidence below comes from different types of sources—government agencies, scientific research, industry groups, and investigative reporting. As you read, consider: What does each source actually show? What are its limitations? And what position does the full body of evidence best support?

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one shows, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
World Health Organization — Antimicrobial Resistance Fact Sheet (2023)
International public health agency

The World Health Organization (WHO) identifies antimicrobial resistance as one of the top 10 global public health threats. According to the WHO, resistant infections directly cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year worldwide and contribute to nearly 5 million more.

The WHO states that resistance is accelerated by the overuse and misuse of antimicrobials in both human medicine and animal agriculture, as well as by poor infection prevention, lack of clean water and sanitation, and limited access to affordable medicines and diagnostics. The WHO does not identify any single sector as the primary driver. Instead, it describes resistance as a problem that “threatens the effective prevention and treatment of an ever-increasing range of infections” and calls for coordinated action across all sectors.

Key figure: 1.27 million deaths per year are directly caused by antibiotic-resistant infections globally.
2
U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) — Antibiotic Use in Food-Producing Animals
U.S. federal health agency

According to the CDC, approximately 70% of antibiotics sold in the United States that are classified as “medically important”—meaning they are also used to treat infections in humans—are sold for use in food-producing animals such as cattle, pigs, and poultry.

The majority of these antibiotics are not used to treat sick animals. Instead, they are added to feed or water to prevent disease in crowded conditions or, historically, to promote faster growth. The CDC warns that when medically important antibiotics are used in livestock, resistant bacteria can develop in animals and then spread to people through the food supply, through direct contact with animals, or through environmental pathways such as water contaminated by animal waste.

Key figure: ~70% of medically important antibiotics sold in the U.S. are used in food-producing animals, not people.
3
Journal of Antimicrobial Chemotherapy — Hospital Prescribing Practices Study (2022)
Peer-reviewed scientific research

Researchers analyzed antibiotic prescribing records from 120 hospitals across the United States and Europe. They found that between 30% and 50% of antibiotic prescriptions written in hospitals were either unnecessary (the patient did not have a bacterial infection) or inappropriate (the wrong antibiotic was chosen, or the treatment lasted longer than needed).

The study argues that over-prescribing in hospitals is a significant and underappreciated driver of resistance. Unlike agricultural use, hospital antibiotics are given directly to humans—meaning resistant bacteria that develop in hospital settings are already adapted to infect people. The researchers highlight drug-resistant infections such as MRSA and C. difficile as examples of resistance driven primarily by clinical overuse, not agricultural use.

Key figure: 30–50% of antibiotic prescriptions in hospitals were found to be unnecessary or inappropriate.
4
National Cattlemen’s Beef Association — Industry Position Statement
Livestock industry trade organization

The National Cattlemen’s Beef Association (NCBA) states that the livestock industry has made significant progress in reducing antibiotic use. Following the FDA’s 2017 Veterinary Feed Directive—which banned the use of medically important antibiotics for growth promotion in animals—total sales of antibiotics for agricultural use declined by 33% between 2015 and 2020.

The NCBA argues that the antibiotics still used in livestock are necessary for animal welfare: to treat sick animals and to prevent disease outbreaks in herds. The organization contends that resistance in livestock bacteria does not automatically transfer to the bacteria that infect humans, because these are often different species of bacteria with different resistance mechanisms. They point to human clinical overuse as the more direct threat to human health.

Key figure: Sales of antibiotics for use in food-producing animals declined 33% from 2015 to 2020 after FDA regulations.
5
Investigative Report: Antibiotic Runoff and Rural Health
Investigative journalism

An investigative team documented conditions in three rural counties located near large-scale poultry and swine farming operations. They found that waste from these operations—which contains antibiotics, antibiotic-resistant bacteria, and resistance genes—was entering local waterways through runoff, leaking waste lagoons, and land application of manure as fertilizer.

Health records showed that residents in these communities had 2.5 times the rate of antibiotic-resistant urinary tract infections compared to national averages. Notably, most affected residents had no direct contact with livestock. Researchers interviewed for the report said the finding suggests that resistant bacteria can spread to humans through environmental pathways—water and soil—not just through food or direct animal contact. The affected communities were predominantly low-income with limited access to healthcare.

Key figure: Residents near large livestock operations had 2.5x the national rate of resistant urinary tract infections.
6
The Lancet — Global Drivers of Antimicrobial Resistance: A Systematic Review (2023)
Peer-reviewed research (systematic review)

A team of international researchers reviewed 412 studies on the causes of antimicrobial resistance published between 2010 and 2023. Their review identified five major interacting drivers of resistance:

(1) Overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human clinical settings
(2) Widespread antibiotic use in agriculture and livestock production
(3) Poor sanitation, water treatment, and infection control—especially in low-income countries
(4) A lack of new antibiotic development by pharmaceutical companies (no major new class of antibiotic has been developed since the 1980s)
(5) Patient behavior, including not finishing prescribed antibiotic courses and demanding antibiotics for viral infections

The authors concluded that no single sector or behavior can be identified as the primary cause. They argue that resistance is a “systems-level phenomenon” in which multiple drivers interact and reinforce each other. For example, agricultural antibiotic use creates environmental reservoirs (Driver 2), which are worsened by poor sanitation (Driver 3), while the lack of new drugs (Driver 4) means that once resistance emerges, there are fewer treatment alternatives. Framing one sector as the sole cause, the authors write, risks “directing resources toward incomplete solutions.”

Key figure: The review identified 5 major interacting drivers of resistance, with no single dominant cause across the 412 studies reviewed.

A parent advocacy group claims that rising vaccination rates have caused the increase in autism diagnoses. The correlation in the data is real—but is the conclusion correct? Read the scenario and evidence below, then construct your own claim that better explains the pattern in the data.

  • Select evidence from the sources below that supports your claim. You must reference at least two sources.

The Scenario

Read this background before reviewing the evidence.

Parent Advocacy Blog • Posted Online

“The Numbers Don’t Lie: Vaccines and Autism Are Connected”

“In 1990, U.S. children received 8 vaccines before age 2. Today they receive 14. During the same period, autism diagnoses rose from 1 in 2,500 to 1 in 36. You don’t need a medical degree to see the pattern. More vaccines, more autism. Until someone can explain why these lines move together, parents have every right to be concerned.”

Shared 48,000 times • 2,100 comments

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects how people communicate, interact, and process information. It is typically identified in early childhood—often between ages 2 and 4—which coincides with the period when children receive several routine vaccinations. This timing has led some parents to wonder whether vaccines might trigger autism.

The question has been studied extensively. The original 1998 study that claimed a link between the MMR vaccine and autism was retracted after investigators found the lead author had falsified data and had undisclosed financial conflicts of interest. His medical license was revoked. But the idea persists, in part because the correlation in the raw data—more vaccines and more autism diagnoses over the same decades—is real.

The evidence below comes from different types of sources—epidemiological studies, government health data, immunology research, and diagnostic history. As you read, consider: Is there a causal mechanism connecting vaccines to autism? What else changed during the same time period that could explain the rise in diagnoses?

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one shows, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
Danish National Cohort Study — MMR Vaccine and Autism (2019)
Peer-reviewed epidemiological research

Researchers followed 657,461 Danish children born between 1999 and 2010 for over a decade. They compared autism rates between children who received the MMR vaccine and those who did not. The study found no increased risk of autism among vaccinated children. The results held across all subgroups analyzed, including children with autism risk factors (older siblings with autism, premature birth) and across different time periods.

This is one of the largest studies ever conducted on the question. Denmark’s universal healthcare system allowed researchers to track every child in the country, eliminating the selection bias that can affect smaller studies.

Key figure: Among 657,461 children, vaccinated children had no higher rate of autism than unvaccinated children. Hazard ratio: 0.93 (95% CI: 0.85–1.02).
2
CDC — Autism Prevalence Surveillance Data (2000–2023)
U.S. federal health surveillance

The CDC’s Autism and Developmental Disabilities Monitoring Network has tracked autism prevalence since 2000. Reported rates have increased steadily: from 1 in 150 (2000) to 1 in 36 (2023). However, the CDC notes that this increase coincides with three major changes: (1) the diagnostic criteria for autism were broadened significantly in 1994 and again in 2013, (2) public awareness campaigns led more parents to seek evaluation, and (3) schools began screening for autism as part of special education eligibility.

The CDC also reports that the increase is most pronounced in demographic groups that historically had low diagnosis rates—girls, Black and Hispanic children, and children in rural areas—suggesting that improved detection, not a new environmental cause, is driving much of the increase.

Key figure: Autism prevalence increased from 1 in 150 (2000) to 1 in 36 (2023), but the largest increases occurred in previously underdiagnosed populations.
3
Journal of Immunology — Infant Immune Response to Vaccination (2022)
Peer-reviewed immunology research

Immunologists studied how infant immune systems respond to vaccines compared to daily environmental exposures. They found that the antigens (immune-stimulating molecules) in the entire childhood vaccine schedule represent a tiny fraction of what a baby’s immune system handles every day. An infant’s immune system successfully responds to thousands of antigens from bacteria, food, and dust within the first days of life.

The researchers calculated that the current vaccine schedule exposes children to approximately 160 immunological components (antigens), compared to roughly 3,000 antigens in the 1960 vaccine schedule (when fewer vaccines used cruder formulations with more components). Modern vaccines are more targeted. The paper found no plausible immunological mechanism by which vaccines could cause neurodevelopmental changes like autism.

Key figure: Today’s vaccine schedule contains ~160 antigens, down from ~3,000 in the 1960s. More vaccines, but far fewer immunological components.
4
The Lancet Retraction — Wakefield et al. (1998, retracted 2010)
Retracted study / investigative journalism

In 1998, Andrew Wakefield published a paper in The Lancet claiming a link between the MMR vaccine and autism in 12 children. The paper generated worldwide fear and vaccination rates dropped in the UK and elsewhere. However, investigative journalist Brian Deer discovered that Wakefield had been paid £435,643 by a lawyer who was suing vaccine manufacturers—a conflict of interest Wakefield did not disclose.

Further investigation revealed that Wakefield had altered the medical histories of the 12 children in his study to fit his hypothesis. The Lancet fully retracted the paper in 2010, calling it “utterly false.” The UK’s General Medical Council found Wakefield guilty of serious professional misconduct and revoked his medical license. No subsequent study has replicated his findings. At least 14 studies involving millions of children have found no link.

Key figure: The original study included only 12 children, had falsified data, and undisclosed financial conflicts. At least 14 subsequent studies involving millions of children found no link.
5
JAMA Pediatrics — Prenatal Brain Development and Autism (2023)
Peer-reviewed neuroscience research

Brain imaging and genetic studies have shown that the neurological differences associated with autism begin developing during pregnancy—months before any vaccines are given. Researchers using MRI scans of infants found that children later diagnosed with autism showed detectable differences in brain structure and connectivity as early as 6 months of age. The biological foundation of autism is established before birth, during prenatal brain development in the second and third trimesters.

Genetic studies have identified over 100 genes associated with autism risk. Twin studies show that autism is 60–90% heritable. Environmental factors (such as advanced parental age, prenatal exposure to certain medications, and pregnancy complications) also play a role, but these factors act during fetal development, not after birth.

Key figure: Brain differences associated with autism are detectable by 6 months of age, and 60–90% of autism risk is genetic—established before any vaccines are given.
6
Japan Natural Experiment — MMR Vaccine Withdrawal (1993–2005)
Epidemiological natural experiment

Japan stopped using the combined MMR vaccine in 1993 due to concerns about a specific mumps component (unrelated to autism). For over a decade, Japanese children received individual measles, mumps, and rubella vaccines instead of the combined shot—or in some cases, no MMR-type vaccine at all. If the MMR vaccine caused autism, diagnoses should have decreased after the vaccine was withdrawn.

Instead, autism diagnoses in Japan continued to rise at the same rate as in countries that kept using MMR. This natural experiment provides strong evidence that the MMR vaccine is not causing the increase in autism diagnoses, since removing the vaccine did not change the trend.

Key figure: After Japan withdrew the MMR vaccine in 1993, autism diagnoses continued to rise—from 48 per 10,000 (1988) to 161 per 10,000 (1996).

The Scenario

Read this background before reviewing the evidence.

Corporate Press Release • GreenPath Industries

“GreenPath Achieves Carbon Neutrality”

“GreenPath Industries is proud to announce that we are now carbon neutral. Through our partnership with forest preservation projects in South America and Southeast Asia, we have purchased carbon offsets equivalent to 100% of our annual emissions. Every product you buy from GreenPath has zero net climate impact.”

Filed with the SEC as part of GreenPath’s 2024 annual sustainability report.

Carbon offsets allow companies to compensate for their greenhouse gas emissions by paying for projects that reduce or remove carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the atmosphere. Common offset projects include planting or preserving forests, capturing methane from landfills, and distributing clean cookstoves in developing countries. Companies purchase offset credits, where one credit typically represents one metric ton of CO₂ either removed or prevented from entering the atmosphere.

The global carbon offset market reached $2 billion in 2023. Many of the world’s largest companies—airlines, tech firms, oil companies—have used offsets to support “carbon neutral” or “net zero” claims. But a growing body of scientific evidence questions whether these offsets deliver the carbon reductions they promise.

The science of carbon sequestration—how carbon is absorbed and stored by ecosystems—is well established. The question is whether offset projects actually produce additional carbon reductions beyond what would have happened anyway, and whether the stored carbon stays sequestered long enough to matter.

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one shows, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
Science — Satellite Analysis of Forest Carbon Offset Projects (2023)
Peer-reviewed earth science research

Researchers used satellite imagery and remote sensing data to evaluate 26 major forest preservation offset projects that had sold over 89 million carbon credits. They compared deforestation rates inside the offset project boundaries to matched control areas with similar characteristics (terrain, accessibility, land tenure) that had no offset protection.

The study found that the projects had avoided only 5.4% of the deforestation they claimed. In other words, 94.6% of the carbon credits sold by these projects represented forests that were not actually at risk of being cut down. The forests would have remained standing even without the offset project. The authors described this as a fundamental “additionality” failure—the offsets were not producing carbon reductions in addition to what would have occurred normally.

Key figure: 94.6% of forest preservation carbon credits analyzed did not represent actual avoided deforestation.
2
IPCC AR6 — Carbon Cycle and Sequestration Timescales (2023)
International scientific assessment

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) explains the fundamental chemistry of the carbon cycle. When fossil fuels are burned, carbon that was stored underground for millions of years is released as CO₂ into the atmosphere in seconds. This carbon remains in the atmosphere for 300–1,000 years.

By contrast, carbon stored in forests is vulnerable to re-release: wildfires, drought, disease, and logging can return stored carbon to the atmosphere within hours. A tree absorbs CO₂ over decades, but a wildfire can release it in minutes. The IPCC notes that this fundamental timescale asymmetry means that biological carbon storage (forests, soil) cannot fully substitute for reducing fossil fuel emissions, because biological storage is inherently temporary and reversible, while fossil fuel emissions are effectively permanent on human timescales.

Key figure: Fossil fuel CO₂ persists in the atmosphere for 300–1,000 years. Forest carbon can be re-released in hours by wildfire.
3
Nature Climate Change — Wildfires and Carbon Offset Reversals (2023)
Peer-reviewed climate science research

Researchers tracked the fate of carbon stored in U.S. forest offset projects registered with California’s cap-and-trade program. They found that wildfires between 2020 and 2023 had already released CO₂ equivalent to the carbon credits from several major projects. One project in Oregon lost 95% of its stored carbon in a single fire season.

The study calculated that the “buffer pool” maintained by offset registries—a reserve of extra credits meant to insure against losses—was insufficient to cover actual wildfire losses. As climate change increases wildfire frequency and intensity, the risk of reversal grows. The authors noted a feedback loop: climate change makes wildfires worse, which destroys carbon offset forests, which fails to reduce emissions, which worsens climate change.

Key figure: Wildfires from 2020–2023 destroyed carbon equivalent to entire offset projects. One Oregon project lost 95% of stored carbon in a single fire season.
4
Verra (Carbon Credit Registry) — Impact Report (2024)
Offset industry registry report

Verra, the world’s largest carbon credit registry, manages the Verified Carbon Standard (VCS). In its 2024 report, Verra states that it has certified over 1,800 projects across 90 countries, representing 1 billion metric tons of CO₂ reductions. Verra emphasizes that its methodology requires projects to demonstrate additionality—that the emissions reductions would not have happened without offset funding.

Verra acknowledged criticism from academic studies but argued that satellite-based comparisons are “overly simplistic” because they do not account for future deforestation pressure: forests that are safe today may not be safe in 10 years without ongoing protection. Verra also pointed to non-forest projects (methane capture, clean energy) that have clearer additionality and are growing as a share of the market.

Key figure: Verra has certified 1 billion metric tons of CO₂ reductions across 1,800 projects. Non-forest offset projects are growing as a share of the market.
5
Global Carbon Project — Annual Emissions Report (2024)
International scientific consortium

The Global Carbon Project tracks worldwide CO₂ emissions from fossil fuels and land use change. In 2023, global fossil fuel emissions reached a record 36.8 billion metric tons of CO₂. The entire voluntary carbon offset market covers approximately 150–200 million metric tons per year—roughly 0.5% of total fossil fuel emissions.

The report notes that even if every offset credit represented a real, permanent carbon reduction (which the evidence above suggests they do not), offsets cover less than 1% of what needs to be eliminated. The science is clear that limiting warming to 1.5°C requires absolute emission reductions of 43% by 2030, which cannot be achieved through offsets alone.

Key figure: Carbon offsets cover ~0.5% of global fossil fuel emissions. Limiting warming to 1.5°C requires a 43% reduction in actual emissions by 2030.
6
Nature Geoscience — Soil Carbon Sequestration Potential and Limits (2023)
Peer-reviewed biogeochemistry research

Researchers studied the chemistry of carbon storage in soils and forests. They found that while ecosystems can absorb CO₂ through photosynthesis, the total global capacity for biological carbon sequestration is limited. The world’s forests and soils together absorb approximately 12 billion metric tons of CO₂ per year—but they also release approximately 11 billion metric tons through decomposition and respiration, for a net absorption of about 1 billion metric tons per year.

This natural carbon sink is already operating near its maximum capacity. Climate change is reducing its effectiveness: warmer temperatures accelerate decomposition (releasing stored carbon faster) and increase drought and wildfire (destroying stored carbon). The researchers concluded that biological sequestration is a valuable part of climate strategy but cannot substitute for reducing fossil fuel emissions at their source.

Key figure: Global ecosystems absorb ~12 Gt CO₂/year but release ~11 Gt, for a net sink of only ~1 Gt/year—less than 3% of fossil fuel emissions.

The Scenario

Read this background before reviewing the evidence.

Grocery Chain Marketing Campaign • FreshHarvest Markets

“Choose Organic. Choose Health.”

“Our customers who buy organic produce report 35% fewer chronic health problems than customers who buy conventional produce. Conventional farming drenches your food in toxic pesticides—over 1 billion pounds of pesticides are applied to U.S. crops every year. FreshHarvest organic produce is grown without synthetic pesticides, so you can feed your family food that’s truly safe. Organic costs a little more, but your health is worth it.”

FreshHarvest Markets — In-store signage and digital marketing campaign, 2024.

The organic food market has grown from $11 billion in 2004 to over $67 billion in 2023 in the United States alone. Consumer surveys consistently show that the top reason people buy organic is the belief that it is healthier and safer. Organic certification prohibits most synthetic pesticides, though it does allow certain naturally derived pesticides.

FreshHarvest’s data is real—their organic customers do report better health. But is this because organic produce is healthier, or because the people who buy organic are different from those who don’t in ways that independently affect health? Understanding the answer requires knowledge of toxicology, dose-response relationships, and the difference between pesticide detection and pesticide harm.

The evidence below comes from agricultural chemistry, toxicology, nutrition science, and consumer behavior research. As you read, consider: Does the presence of a chemical mean it’s causing harm? And what else differs between organic and conventional food consumers?

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one shows, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
USDA Pesticide Data Program — Annual Residue Report (2024)
U.S. federal government testing program

The USDA tests thousands of food samples annually for pesticide residues. In its most recent report, detectable pesticide residues were found on 70% of conventional produce samples and 17% of organic samples. Organic produce does have measurably lower pesticide levels—this is not disputed.

However, the USDA also reports that 99.5% of conventional produce samples had residue levels below the EPA’s tolerance levels (the maximum amount allowed by law). The tolerance levels themselves are set at 100–1,000 times below the lowest dose that caused any observable health effect in laboratory animal studies. In other words, the pesticide residues on conventional produce are typically hundreds of times below the threshold where any biological effect has ever been measured.

Key figure: 99.5% of conventional produce had pesticide residues below EPA tolerance levels, which are already set 100–1,000x below the lowest dose causing any effect in animal studies.
2
Annals of Internal Medicine — Systematic Review: Organic vs. Conventional Health Outcomes (2023 Update)
Peer-reviewed medical research (systematic review)

Researchers updated the landmark Stanford systematic review, analyzing 55 studies comparing health outcomes between people who eat organic and conventional food. The review found no clinically meaningful difference in health outcomes between organic and conventional food consumers after controlling for overall diet quality, lifestyle, and socioeconomic status.

The studies did confirm that organic produce has lower pesticide residues and that organic meat has lower antibiotic-resistant bacteria rates. But these measurable differences did not translate into detectable health outcome differences. The authors noted that the health gap observed in uncontrolled studies (like FreshHarvest’s data) disappears when you control for confounding variables—specifically, diet quality, exercise, smoking, income, and healthcare access.

Key figure: Across 55 studies, no clinically meaningful health difference was found between organic and conventional food consumers after controlling for lifestyle and socioeconomic factors.

Computer scientists explain the mathematical mechanism behind AI bias. Machine learning models find statistical patterns in training data and use those patterns to make predictions. The technical term is representation bias: the model learns to represent the world as its training data represents it. If the data is skewed, the model is skewed. The researchers proved mathematically that without explicit fairness constraints, accuracy-maximizing models will always perform worse on underrepresented groups.

Company Press Release • BrainSprout Learning

BrainSprout Learning, Inc. — Marketing materials and investor presentation, 2024. Subscription: $14.99/month.

Educational technology for young children is a $5 billion market. Companies routinely claim that their products improve cognitive development, often citing brain imaging data to lend scientific authority. Parents, wanting the best for their children, are understandably drawn to products that claim to “build brains.”

BrainSprout’s data appears compelling: children who use the app really do show different brain development measurements. But the study has critical flaws that require understanding how brain imaging works, what cortical thickness actually measures, and what confounding variables separate the families who subscribe to $15/month educational apps from those who don’t.

The evidence below comes from developmental neuroscience, brain imaging methodology, and education research. As you read, consider: What does “cortical thickness growth” actually measure? What else differs between the children in BrainSprout’s study groups?

Evidence

Review all six sources. Consider what each one shows, where it comes from, and what it does not tell you.

1
Nature Neuroscience — Cortical Development in Early Childhood (2023)
Peer-reviewed developmental neuroscience

Neuroscientists explain that cortical thickness—the measurement BrainSprout uses—changes naturally during childhood as part of normal brain development. Between ages 3 and 7, cortical thickness increases in regions associated with language, reading readiness, and executive function. This increase is driven primarily by genetics, nutrition, sleep, physical activity, and the richness of a child’s overall environment (conversations, reading, play, exploration).

The researchers emphasize that cortical thickness is a gross structural measurement, not a direct measure of intelligence or learning. A thicker cortex is not necessarily a “better” cortex—later in childhood, cortical thinning (pruning of unused neural connections) is actually a sign of healthy development. Interpreting cortical thickness changes as “faster brain development” is a significant oversimplification of how the brain matures.

Key figure: Cortical thickness naturally increases in early childhood and is driven primarily by genetics, nutrition, sleep, and environmental richness—not any single activity or product.
2
JAMA Pediatrics — The ABCD Study: Screen Time and Brain Structure in 11,000 Children (2023 Update)
Peer-reviewed longitudinal neuroscience study

The Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) study is the largest long-term brain development study ever conducted in the U.S., tracking 11,878 children with regular MRI scans and cognitive assessments. In its most recent analysis, researchers found that screen time (including educational apps) accounted for less than 0.5% of the variance in brain structure measurements.

The factors that accounted for the most variance in brain development were: (1) genetics/family history (47%), (2) socioeconomic status and household income (12%), (3) sleep duration and quality (8%), (4) physical activity (6%), and (5) parent-child interaction quality (5%). Screen time of all types—educational and recreational—had one of the smallest measurable effects. The researchers concluded that “the effect of screen time on brain development, while statistically detectable in very large samples, is not clinically meaningful.”

Key figure: In 11,878 children, screen time explained <0.5% of brain structure variance. Genetics (47%), income (12%), sleep (8%), exercise (6%), and parent interaction (5%) were far larger factors.
3
Psychological Science — Socioeconomic Status and Cortical Thickness (2024)
Peer-reviewed developmental psychology research

Researchers found a strong, well-documented relationship between household income and children’s cortical thickness. Children from higher-income families show greater cortical thickness in language and executive function regions—the same regions BrainSprout claims its app develops. The mechanism is not money itself but what money provides: better nutrition, more books, safer neighborhoods (less chronic stress), more enrichment activities, and more adult conversation time.

The study found that family income predicted cortical thickness 24 times more strongly than any single activity, including screen time. This creates a critical confounding variable for BrainSprout’s study: families that subscribe to a $15/month educational app are wealthier on average than families that don’t. Their children’s brain development differences likely reflect their overall enriched environment, not the app specifically.

Key figure: Household income predicts cortical thickness 24x more strongly than any single activity. Families who buy educational apps are wealthier, creating a confounding variable.
4
NeuroImage — fMRI Methodology: What Brain Scans Can and Cannot Tell Us (2023)
Peer-reviewed neuroimaging methodology research

Neuroimaging experts explain the limitations of using MRI to evaluate educational products. MRI measures brain structure (size, thickness, volume) but cannot directly measure brain function (learning, memory, intelligence). A structural change does not mean a functional improvement. The brain changes in response to many activities—London taxi drivers have larger hippocampi from memorizing routes, and jugglers show cortical thickening in visual-motor areas—but these structural changes do not necessarily generalize to other cognitive abilities.

The researchers also identify a statistical problem common in small brain imaging studies: with millions of data points per scan, there are many opportunities for statistical noise to produce apparent “significant” results (the multiple comparisons problem). BrainSprout’s study scanned 2,400 children and reported differences in specific brain regions, but did not adequately correct for the number of comparisons made. The study was also not pre-registered, meaning the researchers could have selected whichever brain regions showed differences after looking at the data.

Key figure: MRI measures brain structure, not function. Structural changes do not necessarily mean cognitive improvement. Small studies without pre-registration and multiple comparison correction are prone to false positives.
5
American Academy of Pediatrics — Media Use in Children Under 6 (2024 Update)
Professional medical organization

The AAP recommends that children ages 2–5 be limited to 1 hour per day of “high-quality programming” and that children under 2 avoid screens entirely (except video calling). The AAP notes that some interactive educational media can support learning when it is well-designed, age-appropriate, and used alongside a caregiver (co-viewing).

However, the AAP explicitly warns against marketing claims that apps “build brains” or “accelerate development.” The AAP states: “No app or digital tool has been shown to be superior to, or a substitute for, the learning that occurs through human interaction, play, physical activity, and sleep.” The 2+ hours/day usage that BrainSprout promotes exceeds the AAP’s recommended limit and, if it displaces physical play, outdoor time, or caregiver interaction, could be counterproductive.

Key figure: AAP recommends max 1 hour/day of high-quality screen time for ages 2–5. BrainSprout promotes 2+ hours/day. No app has been shown to outperform human interaction, play, or sleep for brain development.
6
Independent Review of BrainSprout’s Study Methodology (2024)
Independent research methodology review

An independent methodologist reviewed BrainSprout’s published study and identified several critical flaws: (1) The study was not a randomized controlled trial—it compared children whose parents chose to use BrainSprout with children whose parents did not, creating self-selection bias. (2) No controls were included for household income, parental education, number of books in the home, or other enrichment activities. (3) The “23% faster neural development” figure was based on a cherry-picked brain region; other regions showed no difference or showed less development in the app group.

The reviewer also noted that the study was funded entirely by BrainSprout and conducted by researchers with equity stakes in the company. No independent replication has been conducted. The reviewer estimated that after controlling for socioeconomic status and overall enrichment, the app’s independent contribution to cortical thickness would be “indistinguishable from zero.”

Key figure: The study was not randomized, did not control for income or enrichment, cherry-picked one brain region, and was funded by the company. The “23%” claim has not been independently replicated.
