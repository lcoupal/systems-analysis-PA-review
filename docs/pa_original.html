Performance Assessment Design

Assessment Overview

Students demonstrate competency by analyzing a real-world scenario through scientific reasoning and systems thinking. The assessment has two parts: in Part 1, students evaluate a given scientific claim by identifying system components, feedback loops, and stakeholder impacts. In Part 2, students construct their own evidence-based claim using the Claim–Evidence–Reasoning (CER) framework with a different case study and prepared materials.

Modality Performance Assessment
Level Level 2: Explanation and Straightforward Application
Artifact Systems Analysis Brief (interactive HTML form exported as PDF)
Resources Open resource (notes, web, course materials allowed)
Time Untimed (designed to be completable in 60–90 minutes)
Structure

Part 1 (Steps 1–4): Evaluate a given claim using a selected case study

Part 2 (Step 5): Construct own claim using a different case study with prepared materials

Two-Part Assessment Structure

The assessment is divided into two collapsible parts. Students select independently for each part and are not locked to the same domain across both.

Part 1: Evaluate a Given Claim (Steps 1–4)

Students choose one domain, which auto-selects a single case study with an embedded scenario, claim, and 3 curated evidence sources. They then work through Steps 1–4 evaluating that claim. The stakeholder table in Step 4 pre-populates with domain-appropriate examples that students can edit, remove, or add to.

Each domain has one case with a pre-written scenario, claim, and 3 curated evidence sources for students to evaluate. Sources include peer-reviewed research, meta-analyses, and government/institutional reports representing different perspectives on the claim.

Part 2: Construct Your Own Claim (Step 5 — CER)

Students select a different case study from a dropdown organized by domain. Each case includes 6 evidence sources, data tables/figures, a scenario, and a task box. Students construct their own evidence-based claim and support it with evidence and reasoning. Two cases are flagged as “Spurious” to test students’ ability to identify misleading correlations.

Part 1 Evidence Sources

Each Part 1 case includes 3 curated evidence sources that render alongside the scenario. These provide a shared evidence base for evaluation without turning Part 1 into a second CER task. Sources include at least one that supports the claim and one that complicates or challenges it.

Healthcare: Screen Time & Mental Health

Claim: “Increased social media use is associated with higher rates of anxiety and depression in teenagers.”

Source 1: Journal of Adolescent Health — Longitudinal study (n=6,595). Teens using social media >3 hrs/day had 2x depression risk, but social media explained only ~4% of variance. Sleep loss and cyberbullying were the mediating mechanisms.

Source 2: Nature Human Behaviour — Meta-analysis of 226 studies (275,000+ participants). Effect size r = −0.10, far smaller than sleep (r = −0.45), social connection (r = −0.38), or family conflict (r = −0.35).

Source 3: Surgeon General’s Advisory (2023). Called social media a “profound risk of harm” but acknowledged benefits for marginalized youth and noted evidence “does not yet establish causation.”

Stakeholder examples: Teenagers, Parents/guardians, Social media companies

Business: Fast Fashion & Sustainability

Claim: “Our new recycled fabric line reduces environmental impact by 40% compared to conventional materials.”

Source 1: Nature Reviews Earth & Environment — Life cycle analysis. Recycled polyester cuts production emissions 32%, but total lifecycle impact is nearly identical because washing (microfiber release) and disposal dominate.

Source 2: Ellen MacArthur Foundation — Less than 1% of clothing is recycled into new clothing. Some brands launch “sustainable” lines while increasing total production volume.

Source 3: European Environment Agency — 59% of sustainability claims by major fashion brands were unsubstantiated or misleading. 87% of discarded clothing is incinerated or landfilled.

Stakeholder examples: Consumers, Garment workers, Fashion brands

Education: Later School Start Times

Claim: “Starting high school at 8:30 AM or later improves student health and academic performance.”

Source 1: Sleep — Meta-analysis of 29 studies (300,000+ students). Later start times added ~25 min sleep, reduced teen car accidents 16.5%, improved GPA 0.10–0.15 points. Health benefits stronger than academic gains.

Source 2: RAND Corporation — Economic analysis. $9.3B/year in benefits nationally, but $150K–$1.8M per district for transportation restructuring. Benefits grow over time; costs are upfront.

Source 3: Journal of School Health — Equity study. In 8 of 12 districts, later high school start pushed elementary start earlier. Low-income families with young children were disproportionately burdened by the schedule swap.

Stakeholder examples: High school students, Working parents, School districts

IT: AI-Generated Content & Misinformation

Claim: “AI-generated images are becoming increasingly difficult for humans to identify as fake.”

Source 1: Science — Detection study (n=4,000). Humans identified AI-generated faces correctly 48.2% of the time—worse than a coin flip. AI faces rated more trustworthy than real faces. Brief training improved accuracy to 59%.

Source 2: MIT Media Lab — Tested 7 AI detection tools. Best: 85% on known models, 61% on new models. False positive rates 2.3x higher for Black faces. Simple edits defeat most detectors.

Source 3: Reuters Institute — Survey (94,000 people, 47 countries). 59% concerned about AI fakes but only 11% changed behavior. Media literacy training made people 2.1x more likely to verify content.

Stakeholder examples: News consumers, Content creators, Platform companies

Part 2 Case Study Summaries

Each Part 2 (Step 5) case study includes a scenario, 6 evidence sources, data tables/figures, and a CER task. Below is a summary of each case’s design intent.

Healthcare: Antibiotic Resistance (Standard)

Scenario: A claim that livestock farming is the “primary” driver of antibiotic resistance. Evidence shows multiple interacting drivers (clinical overuse, agriculture, poor sanitation, lack of new drugs). Tests whether students recognize a multi-causal system cannot be reduced to a single cause. Sources: WHO data, CDC reports, peer-reviewed hospital studies, industry position, investigative journalism, Lancet systematic review.

Healthcare: Hospital Visits & Mortality (Spurious)

Scenario: A blog post claims “people who visit hospitals more often have higher mortality rates, so hospitals are making people sicker.” The correlation is real but spurious—sicker people visit hospitals more often. Tests confounding variables and reverse causation. Sources: CDC health statistics, peer-reviewed epidemiology, federal health agency data, healthcare industry association, university medical research, media and communications research.

Business: Remote Work Productivity (Standard)

Scenario: A CEO claims remote workers are 13% more productive. Evidence is mixed: the cited Stanford study found productivity gains but also lower promotion rates. Hybrid models outperform both extremes; monitoring software reduces trust. Tests selective evidence citation. Sources: peer-reviewed Stanford study, corporate research, large-scale employee survey, NBER working paper, professional association research.

Business: Food Delivery & Restaurants (Standard)

Scenario: A delivery platform claims partner restaurants see a 30% order increase. Evidence shows volume up but profit down (8% decline), with commission structures that erode margins. Outcomes vary by restaurant type. Tests selective evidence: what the platform reported vs. what the full evidence shows. Sources: peer-reviewed research, industry trade association, federal statistics, platform company report, municipal policy research, worker advocacy organization.

Education: Class Size & Student Achievement (Standard)

Scenario: A school board wants to spend $4.2M to cut class sizes from 28 to 15, citing the STAR study. California’s $1.5B attempt failed due to hiring unqualified teachers. NBER data shows tutoring produces 3x the gains per dollar. Tests cost-effectiveness trade-offs. Sources: Tennessee STAR experiment, California policy evaluation, NBER economic research, AFT position statement, OECD PISA data, University of Chicago school research.

Education: Technology in the Classroom (Standard)

Scenario: An ed-tech company claims 22% test score improvement. Independent review found it shrinks to 3–5% when properly controlled. OECD shows moderate tech helps but heavy use hurts. Tests methodology flaws (survivorship bias, no control group) and the digital divide. Sources: OECD international research, MIT/Harvard RCT, Journal of Educational Psychology, U.S. Dept of Education, independent review, Pew digital divide report.

IT: Social Media Algorithms (Standard)

Scenario: A social media VP testifies “less than 1% harmful content.” Evidence shows 0.7% of 200B daily views = ~1.4B harmful views/day. MIT found false news spreads 70% more. Meta’s leaked research showed algorithm amplified divisive content. Tests scale reasoning (percentages vs. absolute numbers) and corporate transparency claims. Sources: MIT Sloan research, leaked Meta documents, Nature randomized experiment, Surgeon General’s advisory, Reuters Institute, platform transparency report.

IT: Password Policies & Security Breaches (Spurious)

Scenario: A tech blogger claims companies with stricter password policies have 3.2x more breaches. Confounded by company size and detection capability. When controlled for size, the correlation reverses. Sources: Verizon breach report, NIST digital identity guidelines, Carnegie Mellon user behavior research, IBM breach cost report, Google auth research, independent re-analysis.

Scoring Weight Options

This section presents options for how evidence statements are weighted within each competency and how competencies relate to overall assessment scoring. These are discussion options for the CCW, not final decisions.

C1 — Evaluates Claims (3 ES)

All three ES live in Part 1 only (Step 1). They are naturally balanced across claim evaluation tasks.

Option ES1 (components) ES2 (distinguish) ES3 (quality)
Equal 33% 33% 34%

Recommendation: Equal at ~33% each. All three are assessed in the same step and contribute equally to claim evaluation.

C2 — Supports Claims (3 ES)

All three ES live in Part 2 only (Step 5). The question is whether constructing the claim (ES1) should weigh less than supporting it (ES2) and defending it (ES3).

Option ES1 (make claim) ES2 (support) ES3 (counterclaim)
Equal 33% 33% 34%
Support-heavy 20% 45% 35%

Recommendation: Equal at ~33% each. Making a claim is the foundation; supporting it and defending it are equally important. All three are part of the same CER workflow.

C3 — Explains Systems (4 ES)

ES1 is assessed once (Step 2). ES2 is assessed twice (Steps 2, 3). ES3 is assessed twice (Step 3 + Step 5). ES4 is assessed once (Step 4).

Option ES1 (components) ES2 (interactions) ES3 (science) ES4 (societal)
Equal 25% 25% 25% 25%
Systems-heavy 20% 30% 30% 20%

Recommendation: Equal at 25% each. ES2 and ES3 already get more assessment surface area (assessed in multiple steps). Equal weighting keeps it clean.

Competency-Level Weight Options

Three approaches for how the three competencies relate to overall assessment scoring:

Approach C1 C2 C3 Logic
Equal 33% 33% 34% Simple; each comp matters equally
Systems-heavy 25% 25% 50% C3 has 4 ES and spans both parts; systems thinking is the course focus
Pass/fail per comp — — — No overall weight; student must meet threshold on each comp independently

Recommendation: Pass/fail per comp with equal ES weights within each comp. This is the cleanest model for a performance assessment—it means a student can’t compensate for weak systems thinking with strong CER construction. Each competency stands on its own. This aligns with WGU’s mastery model.

Design Rationale

Choose Your Own Adventure: Per CCW guidance, students select a domain and scenario that interests them. Part 1 and Part 2 selections are independent.

Two-Part Structure: Part 1 (evaluate) develops foundational skills of recognizing good and bad claims. Part 2 (construct) requires students to build their own claim with CER. This follows the KSA progression: knowledge → skill → ability.

Clean Competency Separation: C1 (Evaluates Claims) lives entirely in Part 1. C2 (Supports Claims) lives entirely in Part 2. C3 (Explains Systems) is primarily Part 1 with reinforcement in Part 2. Each competency has a clear home, making scoring straightforward.

Original cases: The content is real-world policy/business/tech. But students apply scientific reasoning methods — evaluating evidence quality, identifying confounding variables, distinguishing correlation from causation, assessing source credibility, constructing evidence-based claims.

Part 1 Evidence Anchoring: Each Part 1 case includes 3 curated sources (fewer than Part 2’s 6) to give students a shared evidence base for evaluation without turning Part 1 into a second CER task. Stakeholder tables pre-populate with domain-appropriate examples that students can edit.

Prepared Materials for CER: Step 5 provides 6 evidence sources, data tables, and figures so students have a body of work to draw from. This ensures evaluators do not need domain expertise and that all students have equal access to evidence. Sources represent opposing viewpoints, requiring students to synthesize rather than cherry-pick.

Counterclaim Requirement: Students must identify the strongest argument against their own claim and explain why their position still holds. This directly assesses C2-ES3 and mirrors the structure of peer review.

Spurious Correlation Cases: Two cases (Hospital Visits & Mortality, Password Policies & Breaches) present real correlations with misleading causal claims. Students must identify confounding variables or alternative explanations and construct a claim that better explains the data.

No Drawing Required: Students describe system components and feedback loops in writing.

Embedded Scenarios: Full scenario context and claim are displayed after selection.

Process Over Perfection: Focus on the right way to think and communicate, not finding the right answer.

Open Resource: Students can use notes, web, and course materials.

Full ES Coverage: All 10 evidence statements across 3 competencies are assessed through explicit prompts. The Combined Coverage matrix confirms no gaps.
